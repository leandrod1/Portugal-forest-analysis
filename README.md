# Portugal Forest and Wildfire Analysis

## Project Description

This project explores the relationship between forest composition and wildfire patterns in Portugal over the last 30 years. Using official land use and burned area datasets, the analysis focuses on the evolution of the country’s dominant forest species: Eucalyptus (*Eucalyptus globulus*) and its potential role in wildfire occurrence and spread.

The expansion of eucalyptus in Portugal has long been part of the public and political debate surrounding wildfires. This study approaches the topic through spatial and temporal data analysis to better understand whether this species can be considered a key driver of wildfires, or whether the phenomenon is better explained by a broader set of environmental and socio-economic factors.

## Data Sources

This project uses official geospatial and wildfire datasets from Portugal:

* **COS (1995 & 2023)** — Land use and land cover data from the Direção-Geral do Território (DGT)
* **ICNF Burned Area Records (1996–2025)** — Historical wildfire data from the Instituto da Conservação da Natureza e das Florestas (ICNF)

## Research Questions

* How has Portugal’s forest composition changed between 1995 and 2023?
* To what extent has eucalyptus expanded across the country?
* How do wildfire patterns relate to different forest types?
* Are eucalyptus plantations more strongly associated with wildfire occurrence than other forest species?

## Methodology

The analysis combines:

* Spatial comparison of land cover maps (1995 vs. 2023)
* Burned area overlays using wildfire cartography (1996–2025)
* Forest type distribution analysis
* Regional wildfire hotspot mapping
* Temporal trend analysis of burned forest areas

## Key Insights

* Eucalyptus has become the most widespread forest species in Portugal, replacing large areas previously occupied by maritime pine.
* Maritime pine and eucalyptus represent similar proportions of total burned forest area.
* The expansion of eucalyptus is strongly linked to economic profitability and the pulp industry.
* Wildfire occurrence in Portugal is strongly influenced by multiple factors, including land abandonment, fuel accumulation, weather conditions, and forest management practices.

## Final Thoughts

The results suggest that while eucalyptus should not be considered the sole cause of wildfires in Portugal, its biological characteristics and large-scale monoculture expansion contribute to wildfire propagation under certain conditions. A transition toward more diverse forest systems, including oak and chestnut species, combined with active land management and economic support for landowners, could improve landscape resilience and reduce wildfire risk.
